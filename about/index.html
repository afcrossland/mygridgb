About MyGridGB

Welcome to MyGridGB

The way that electricity is being generated in Great Britain changes every minute of every day. MyGridGB provides real-time information and analysis of energy generation and carbon emissions across Great Britain — making the story of Britain's energy transition easy to understand, whether you're a policymaker, researcher, student, or just curious about where your electricity comes from.

The platform tracks electricity production and consumption across all generating technologies, shows how much carbon is being emitted at any moment, and places it all in the context of Britain's decarbonisation targets.

MyGridGB also charts carbon emissions from electricity — of critical importance for a country with legally binding carbon reduction targets — and includes a Clean Power 2030 simulation that models what the GB grid could look like under different combinations of power station technologies.

Carbon Targets

The assumed 2030 carbon target used on this site is 50–100 grams of CO₂ equivalent per kWh, based on analysis by the Committee on Climate Change. MyGridGB tracks progress against this in real time. The carbon intensity figures use life-cycle emissions estimates based on IPCC reference data, capturing the full carbon cost of each generation type rather than combustion emissions alone.

Data Sources

Generation & Grid Data

Electricity generation and transmission data comes from BM Reports via Elexon — the electricity trading body for England and Wales. Data updates every 5 minutes. Note: as of November 2019, embedded wind estimates are no longer included in grid generation figures.

Solar Data

Solar generation is provided by the Sheffield Solar PVLive service at the University of Sheffield, aggregated from approximately 20,000 UK solar installations. Sheffield Solar also provides PV_Regional and PV_Forecast services to National Grid and the energy industry.

Carbon Intensity

Carbon intensity is calculated using life-cycle emissions factors for each generating technology, based on IPCC median reference values. See the full table below.

2030 Grid Simulation

The Clean Power 2030 plan simulator uses publicly available capacity assumptions to model what different combinations of generation technologies would produce and their associated carbon impact.
