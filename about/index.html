About MyGridGB

Welcome to MyGridGB

MyGridGB provides real-time information and analysis of energy and carbon emissions in Great Britain. The site was created to make the story of Britain's energy transition easy to understand — whether you're a policymaker, researcher, student, or just curious about where your electricity comes from.

The platform tracks electricity production and consumption across all generating technologies, shows how much carbon is being emitted at any moment, and places it all in the context of Britain's decarbonisation targets. You can come back any time to see how the grid is changing.

MyGridGB also includes a Clean Power 2030 simulation that models what the GB grid could look like under different combinations of power station technologies.

Carbon Targets

The UK Committee on Climate Change has set a target of 50–100 grams of CO₂ equivalent per kWh for British electricity by 2030. MyGridGB tracks progress against this in real time. The carbon intensity figures shown on this site use life-cycle emissions estimates based on IPCC reference data.

Data Sources

Generation & Grid Data

Electricity generation and transmission data comes from BM Reports via Elexon — the electricity trading body for England and Wales. Data updates every 5 minutes.

Solar Data

Solar generation is provided by the Sheffield Solar PVLive service at the University of Sheffield, aggregated from approximately 20,000 UK solar installations. Regional and forecast data also available via Sheffield Solar.

Carbon Intensity

Carbon intensity is calculated using life-cycle emissions factors for each generating technology, based on IPCC reference values. This captures the full carbon cost of each generation type, not just combustion emissions.

2030 Grid Simulation

The Clean Power 2030 plan simulator uses publicly available capacity assumptions to model what different combinations of generation technologies would produce and their associated carbon impact.

Solar Petition

MyGridGB ran a petition calling for solar panels on all new homes built in the UK. The petition did not reach its target, but the case for rooftop solar in new builds remains strong. Read the full justification.
